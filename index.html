The pitch

I love tech, especially because of its potential to reach a huge mass of people and improve developing economies. I want to grow the tech economy in the Philippines. Product management will help me ship my ideas and give me appropriate training and confidence to start a company of my own. But also, I love how it infuses the things I'm passionate about into one generalist role. I hope through working as a designer, and eventually software engineering, I can get a better understanding of how to transition into product management.

I work in a robotics startup as a designer, also learning basic frontend. I love my role but am frustrated that I do not have enough technical knowledge to help build. Additionally, I love the mindset of the engineers I work with -- never giving up until they solve a problem and I want to carry the same resilience.

To help me in my path, I think it's important I know how to communicate technically, build my resilience as a problem solver and learn how to build things of value to others.

So.. why Horizons?

I discovered my interest in tech late in the game when it was too late to switch majors. Even then, I talked to my CompSci friends about the major and how they wished it was more practical. I love my school, and while theory is suuuper important, I wanted to BUILD THINGS OF VALUE. I had been searching for a way to learn coding or programming in a practical way for a while now-- visited a bunch of bootcamps in SF, read through many reviews online, etc. Horizons appealed to me most because:

A strong and young community. Community is extremely important to me and I think being in this environment of SUPER COOL and passionate kids (based on testimonies from curren students) will push me in unimaginable ways. I admire that it's one of the few programs I know that reels in a younger generation, not just the working population.

More than just coding. 80% of the time coding. 20% of the time meeting AMAZING leaders in the tech industry. There's a strong emphasis on creating the next generation of entrepreneurs, not just people who can write code. What a great investment on young people.

Emphasis on school, less a bootcamp. A big critique of coding bootcamps is that they only teach their students how to get a job. When I was interviewing, I was told that there were classes and lessons that emphasized algorithms and data structures, lessons integral for software engineering. The format may be bootcamp-style, but the "school" part was heavily emphasized.

Greaaaaat timing. I'm interning in a Robotics company doing UX/UI Design, and working with amazing engineers. I love my job, but, despite being the design, HTML and CSS guru, I still have a huge frustration of not understanding the more technical side of things. My coworkers taught me React and Redux, I implemented a feature and it was so fulfilling -- I wanted more! I saw an email about this and I immediately wanted in.

What's the most important turning point in my life thus far?

Leaving home.

When I got the letter about the Freeman Asian Scholarship for Wesleyan, I cried. Not tears of joy. I got the scholarship late and by then I was deadset to study in a really good local university under the Management Engineering track. I was scared of leaving home behind and venturing into this foreign jungle. If I had stayed, I would have probably taken the corporate route. Would have gotten a lot of money, sure. But maybe would have never gotten a taste of what it meant to live out my passion. I am so happy I took the plunge.

My freshman year was DIFICULT! I maybe called my dad up and cried to him almost every week that fall semester. I had to adjust to a new culture (collectivist > individualistic, conservative > liberal), a new lifestyle (HS > college) AND a new school (and a very liberal school at that)!

But, as our diversity leader tells us: "Lean into discomfort." It was hard, but without it, I wouldn't be the empathetic, self-aware person I am today. And I wouldn't have discovered my passion in technology and in uplifting the Philippines. Being away from home only pushed me closer towards it. I saw the possibilities of tech in other countries and now I want to find a way to thread it back to my home, the place I love most.
